A data investigation · 2018–2025

The Ballon d'Or rewards goals.
It also rewards stories.

Every year, football argues the winner was a narrative, not a season. We measured the narrative — and found where it actually hides.

scroll ↓

The accusation

The complaint is always some version of the same thing: that was a story, not a season. The winner had a World Cup, a transfer saga, a fairy-tale rise — and the voters fell for it.

It's satisfying. It's also almost impossible to test, because hype and merit are hopelessly tangled: players get talked about because they produce. So we don't ask "hype vs. merit." We ask something sharper:

Does attention predict the Ballon d'Or after you account for what a player actually did and how their team performed?

If it does, that's the bias. If it doesn't, "they're inseparable" is itself the answer. Here's what seven years of data say.

How we score it

Two numbers do all the work.

Measuring the story

Start with raw attention

We take a player's all-language Wikipedia traffic over their qualifying season — a clean, global measure of how much the world was watching.

Subtract what merit & fame explain

Stars draw traffic every day; great seasons draw more. We model the attention a player's production, baseline fame, and team success should generate.

What's left is the Hype Score

The attention left over — beyond what merit and fame explain — is the story. We call it the Hype Score: attention beyond what a player's season explains. For a 16-year-old Lamine Yamal, almost all of it. The model expected a whisper; he got a roar.

It cuts both ways

Kevin De Bruyne in 2019 is the mirror image: an elite season that drew less attention than his numbers deserved. A negative Hype Score — robbed of the buzz.

The finding

The bias isn't in who wins. It's in who gets considered.

How much the Hype Score moves each gate · bars show the uncertainty range

The award is two decisions

First a 30-player shortlist — do you get noticed? Then a vote that ranks them — do you finish higher? We ask the narrative question at each gate, holding merit and team success fixed.

Gate A — getting noticed

Here narrative matters a lot. Being talked about beyond what you did makes you far likelier to make the 30 — even with merit in the model.

Gate B — finishing higher

Here it matters far less. Once you're in the room, the vote is driven by production and trophies; narrative adds only a modest nudge.

So the bias hides early

Narrative's real power isn't winning the Ballon d'Or. It's getting you onto the list — the quiet decision nobody watches.

The finding, year by year

Best season. Biggest Hype Score. Actual winner.

"Biggest Hype Score" means the most attention beyond what a player's season explains — not simply the most-viewed player (that's almost always Messi or Ronaldo). Because the Hype Score subtracts merit by design, the best player is rarely the most over-hyped one; the point is where the actual winner falls between them.

2018

Modrić won with the highest Hype Score of any winner here — far more attention than his merit (20th in the field) explained — while Messi quietly had the best season. The complaint, confirmed.

2019

Messi won on merit, not noise — among the very best by production and with only modest buzz. No narrative inflation here; a clean one.

2021

The famous one. Lewandowski had the best season in the field by the numbers — a record-breaking goalscorer — with almost no buzz beyond it, and finished second. Messi won on the narrative of a first Copa América and a move to PSG. The story edging the goals.

Benzema took the vote on a Champions League run — while Mbappé out-produced everyone again.

2023

Messi: best season, most votes, and plenty of buzz. Hard to call this one a robbery.

2024

Rodri won ranked 14th on raw production — the holding midfielder whose value the numbers can barely see.

2025

Dembélé won with almost no extra buzz; Salah had the best season of anyone and finished 4th.

The cases everyone argues about

Same production, opposite narrative

Modrić, 2018 — a fine season, a World Cup final, and the highest narrative residual of any winner here. The data agrees with the complaint — with one caveat: Modrić is a deep-lying playmaker, the role our merit reads least well, so part of that residual may be value the stats can't see rather than pure story. Lewandowski, 2019 — one of the best merit scores in seven years, a negative Hype Score, 8th place. The underrated profile, quantified.

Plot merit against attention and the thesis lives in two dots: Messi 2023 and Lewandowski 2019, near-identical elite production — and opposite buzz.

A word on what merit means here. For attackers it's production — goals, assists, the expected-goals build-up. But a holding midfielder like Jorginho — a Champions League and Euro winner in 2021 — does his best work where the box score can't see it. So merit grades each player on the role he actually plays: a ball-winning score (tackles, interceptions, blocks, clearances) for midfielders, a shot-stopping score for keepers, and a tackle/aerial/ball-playing score for center-backs. By that fuller measure Jorginho's 2021 is clearly positive, and his Hype Score is negative — not over-hyped, but quietly under-rated. One honest limit: the purely-positional center-backs (a Rúben Dias) still read low — great positioning means fewer actions to count, and no public stat captures it.

Is it real?

The finding survives the stress tests

With only seven years of data, one model proves nothing. So we re-ran the result dozens of ways — dropping Messi and Ronaldo, dropping breakout newcomers, leaving out each year one at a time. The getting-noticed effect barely budges. The finishing-higher effect is smaller and leans partly on the two superstars — so we say it quietly.

What each stress test means — and whether it changes the answer
main model
The headline result — all the data, with the attention window cut before the shortlist. Everything else is measured against this.
drop Messi & Ronaldo
Refit with the two superstars removed. Getting-noticed barely moves; finishing-higher weakens and its range touches zero — which is why we state that second effect quietly.
drop low-fame players
Remove players with too little prior attention to gauge a fair baseline. No change — the effect isn't an artefact of thin-data players.
window past the ceremony
Deliberately let attention run past the winner announcement, catching the result-day spike. Finishing-higher inflates exactly as a leak should, while getting-noticed is unmoved (the shortlist is decided before the ceremony) — proof the window discipline matters.
window before the ceremony
The opposite guardrail: cut a player's season at the ceremony date so not even one post-ceremony match counts. Both gates barely move — the measurement isn't riding on a few late games.
leave each year out
Refit seven times, dropping one award year each time. Both gates stay stable across all years — no single season is driving the result.

Two more, reported in the full report: a bootstrap that re-estimates the Hype Score on resampled data (so the uncertainty of measuring it is included) keeps getting-noticed far from zero and finishing-higher positive; and a Heckman selection control (modelling who gets nominated first) leaves finishing-higher the same sign. In short: getting-noticed is rock-solid across every test; finishing-higher is real but smaller and leans on the two superstars.

What this is, and isn't

The honest fine print

  • Small sample. Seven award years — this is an interpretation, not a prediction engine. The uncertainty ranges are wide on purpose.
  • Merit covers every role, but not equally well. It grades attackers on production, midfielders on ball-winning, keepers on shot-stopping, and center-backs on tackle/aerial/ball-playing — so defenders and keepers are measured, not ignored. The honest gap: purely-positional center-backs (a Rúben Dias) still read low, because great positioning leaves fewer actions to count and no public stat captures it. So the Hype Score is "attention beyond the merit we can measure" — for those hard-to-measure roles, part of it may be real value the box score misses rather than narrative.
  • One attention signal. Wikipedia pageviews, summed across every language. A second, independent read from news-article volume (GDELT) is a natural cross-check, and is in progress. Pageviews can also reflect a player's national-market size or diaspora; baseline fame absorbs the steady part, but some residual market effect may remain.
  • We only see the nominees. The finishing-higher model studies just the ~30 players who made the shortlist — a hand-picked group, which can bias the result. The textbook fix is a Heckman correction: model who gets nominated first, then adjust the finishing model for it. We name it as the principled next step — and note that our two-gate design already studies that nomination step head-on, which is where the bias turned out to live.

So — goals or stories?

Both, and you can mostly tell them apart — but not where the arguments happen. By the time votes are counted, production and trophies do most of the work. The bias lives one step earlier, in the invisible decision about who even gets to be on the list.

Narrative's real power isn't making you win the Ballon d'Or. It's getting you into the room.

What we mean

Hype Score
Attention beyond what a player's season explains — the buzz left over once production, fame and team success are accounted for.
Merit
How good the season actually was, measured per role: production for attackers, ball-winning for midfielders, shot-stopping for keepers, duels + ball-playing for defenders.
The two gates
The award is two decisions — getting noticed (making the 30-player shortlist) then finishing higher in the vote. We test the buzz at each.
Methods & reproducibility

In plain terms: we measure each player's season (per position), their fame and team success, and their attention (Wikipedia pageviews across every language). The Hype Score is the attention a statistical model can't explain from those. Two models then ask whether that leftover buzz predicts making the shortlist and finishing higher, and the result is re-checked dozens of ways. Years covered: 2018, 2019, 2021–2025 (2020 cancelled; 2017 predates the underlying data). Everything is cache-first and reproducible via python run.py <stage>.
