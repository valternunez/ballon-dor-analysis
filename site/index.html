The Ballon d'Or · 2018–2025

The Ballon d'Or rewards goals — and the stories around them.

Every year, a winner is written off as a narrative rather than a season. This study measures that narrative and locates where it actually changes the result.

The accusation

That was a story, not a season.

The complaint usually runs the same way: the winner had a World Cup, a transfer saga, a fairy-tale rise, and the voters were swayed by it. It is a hard claim to test, because attention and merit are closely tied — players are talked about largely because they produce.

So the question here is not “hype versus merit” but a narrower, conditional one: does attention predict the award after accounting for what a player did and how their team performed?

How we score it

Two measures: merit, and attention beyond it.

01 / Merit

How good the season was.

Each player is graded against others in their own position and season, on the stats that matter for the job — goals and chance-creation for attackers, tackles and interceptions for midfielders, goals prevented for keepers, duels and ball-progression for defenders. It collapses into one number: how far above the pack they were.

02 / Hype Score  H⊥

Attention they didn't earn on the pitch.

We measure how much the world actually watched a player — all-language Wikipedia traffic — then model how much their performance, existing fame and team success should have drawn. The Hype Score is the gap left over: the buzz the numbers can't explain.

In short: merit is what a player did; the Hype Score is the attention that arrived beyond it.

The finding

The bias is in who gets considered, not who wins.

The award is two decisions: a 30-player shortlist (do you get noticed?), then a vote that ranks them (do you finish higher?). We test the narrative at each stage, holding merit and team success fixed. The nomination test covers attacking candidates, where a season's merit is best measured; defenders and keepers are judged at the placement stage.

Narrative's effect on getting noticed is roughly five times its effect on where a nominee places.

Robustness

Does the result hold up?

With seven years of data, a single model settles little, so the result was re-estimated dozens of ways — dropping Messi and Ronaldo, dropping breakout newcomers, leaving out each year in turn.

The getting-noticed effect stays in the same place across every cut. The finishing-higher effect is smaller and leans partly on the two superstars, so it is reported cautiously.

It also holds once we account for how central a player was to his club — his share of the team's minutes and attacking output — so a midfield anchor like Rodri being indispensable is not what the Hype Score is mistaking for buzz.

And it reproduces on a different attention signal: rebuilding the score from global news coverage (not Wikipedia) leaves the getting-noticed effect clearly positive — so the result is not an artifact of one data source.

What this is, and isn't

Limitations.

Small sample.

Seven award years. This is an interpretation rather than a prediction engine, and the uncertainty ranges are deliberately wide.

Every role, not equally well.

Merit grades attackers, midfielders, keepers and defenders — but purely-positional centre-backs still read low, because great positioning leaves fewer actions to count. An independent Opta-based rating agrees with our merit overall (and closely for attackers) yet barely separates defenders either, so this is a limit of public match data, not of our index alone.

One attention signal.

Wikipedia pageviews, summed across every language. Traffic can also track a player's national-market size or diaspora; baseline fame absorbs the steady part, but some residual market effect may survive. A second, independent read from news volume is a natural cross-check, and is in progress.

We only see the nominees.

The finishing model studies only the ~30 who made the shortlist. The two-gate design addresses that selection directly, and the shortlist is where the bias proved strongest.

So — goals or stories?

Narrative's main effect is on who reaches the shortlist — not on who wins it.

By the time votes are counted, production and trophies do most of the work. The bias sits one step earlier, in the quieter decision about who makes the list at all.
