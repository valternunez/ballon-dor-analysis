The accusation

That was a story, not a season.

The complaint is always a version of the same thing: the winner had a World Cup, a transfer saga, a fairy-tale rise — and the voters fell for it. Satisfying, and almost impossible to test, because hype and merit are hopelessly tangled. Players get talked about because they produce.

So we don't ask “hype versus merit.” We ask the sharper, conditional question: does attention predict the award after accounting for what a player did and how their team performed?

How we score it

Two numbers do all the work.

01 / Merit

How good the season was.

Each player is graded against others in their own position and season, on the stats that matter for the job — goals and chance-creation for attackers, tackles and interceptions for midfielders, goals prevented for keepers, duels and ball-progression for defenders. It collapses into one number: how far above the pack they were.

02 / Hype Score  H⊥

Attention they didn't earn on the pitch.

We measure how much the world actually watched a player — all-language Wikipedia traffic — then model how much their performance, existing fame and team success should have drawn. The Hype Score is the gap left over: the buzz the numbers can't explain.

In one line: merit is what a player did; the Hype Score is the attention that arrived beyond it.

The finding

The bias isn't in who wins. It's in who gets considered.

The award is two decisions. A 30-player shortlist — do you get noticed? Then a vote that ranks them — do you finish higher? We test the narrative at each gate, holding merit and team success fixed.

Narrative's pull on getting noticed is roughly five times its pull on who actually places.

The finding, year by year

Best season. Biggest story. Actual winner.

“Biggest story” is the most attention beyond what a season explains — not the most-viewed player. The point is where the winner falls between the two.

Is it real?

It survives the stress tests.

With seven years of data, one model proves nothing. So we re-ran the result dozens of ways — dropping Messi and Ronaldo, dropping breakout newcomers, leaving out each year in turn.

The getting-noticed effect barely moves. The finishing-higher effect is smaller and leans partly on the two superstars — so we say it quietly.

What this is, and isn't

The honest fine print.

Small sample.

Seven award years — an interpretation, not a prediction engine. The uncertainty ranges are wide on purpose.

Every role, not equally well.

Merit grades attackers, midfielders, keepers and defenders — but purely-positional center-backs still read low, because great positioning leaves fewer actions to count.

One attention signal.

Wikipedia pageviews, summed across every language. A second, independent read from news volume is a natural cross-check, and is in progress.

We only see the nominees.

The finishing model studies just the ~30 who made the shortlist. The two-gate design tackles that selection head-on — which is where the bias turned out to live.

So — goals or stories?

Narrative's real power isn't making you win.
It's getting you into the room.

By the time votes are counted, production and trophies do most of the work. The bias lives one step earlier — in the invisible decision about who even gets to be on the list.
